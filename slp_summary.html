Advance Care Planning & Advance Health Directives

Quick reference summary — keep this handy on placement

Foundations

Process

Advance Care Planning

Ongoing process — not a single form

Reflection on values, goals & preferences for future care

Voluntary — start any age, ideally before illness

Review every 2–5 years or when health changes

WA Health framework: Think · Talk · Write · Share

Legal document

Advance Health Directive

Legally binding under G&A Act 1990 (WA)

Records treatment consents & refusals for specific future circumstances

Activates only when: (a) it applies to the treatment AND (b) patient lacks capacity

Validity: ≥18, full capacity, ≥1 treatment decision, 2 witnesses

Cannot include: VAD, futile treatment, organ donation

Clinical form

Goals of Patient Care

Clinical care planning form — hospital & RACF setting

Led by clinical team, not the patient

Guidance only — not legally binding

Complements AHD — does not replace it

May cover situations not anticipated in the AHD

WA Hierarchy of Treatment Decision-Makers (when no AHD exists)

1st Advance Health Directive Must be followed if valid & applicable
2nd Enduring Guardian Appointed by the patient
3rd Guardian with Authority Court or tribunal appointed
4th Spouse / De facto
5th Adult child
6th–8th Parent · Sibling · Carer
Last resort State Administrative Tribunal SAT — sat.justice.wa.gov.au

Decision-Making Capacity

Four-element framework

Assessing capacity — all four must be satisfied

1

Understand — can they understand the information relevant to the decision?

2

Retain — can they hold the information long enough to make a decision?

3

Weigh up — can they reason through the decision and appreciate its consequences?

4

Communicate — can they express their decision in any way?

Key principles

Never forget these

Presumed present — burden is on demonstrating absence

Decision-specific — not a blanket judgement

Time-specific — reassess when circumstances change

Cognitive impairment ≠ no capacity

Document all 4 elements, date & sign

Ethics, grey areas & definitions

Principle

Autonomy is the foundation

A competent adult can refuse any treatment — even life-saving. A valid binding refusal must be followed regardless of family or clinician views.

Grey area

Family cannot override an AHD

Next-of-kin wishes carry no legal precedence over a valid AHD. The document is the patient's voice. Acknowledge distress, but hold firm.

Grey area

The limited exception

Clinician may deviate only if: circumstances changed in an unforeseeable way AND a reasonable person would have decided differently. High bar — not routine.

Definition

What is the SAT?

State Administrative Tribunal — WA's independent body for resolving guardianship & medical treatment disputes. Last resort when AHD validity is contested.

Grey area

AHDs cannot request VAD

Voluntary assisted dying requires ongoing capacity throughout and follows a separate legal pathway. It cannot be pre-authorised in an AHD.

Practice point

Always document disputes

Record the nature of the dispute, who was involved, what the AHD states, why it applies, and steps taken to resolve it. Your notes protect the patient and you.
